Where Sri Lanka's Leopards Thrive?

Within the rich and varied landscapes of Sri Lanka, leopards thrive, adapting to and flourishing in diverse ecosystems across the country.

Horton Plains

Yala National Park

Wilpaththu National Park

Sri Lankan leopard (Panthera pardus kotiya) is a subspecies native to Sri Lanka, featuring a lighter coat with larger, more widely spaced rosettes compared to other leopards.

Classified as endangered, these leopards face threats such as habitat loss, human-wildlife conflict, and poaching, prompting conservation efforts to protect their dwindling population.

Habitat Loss and Fragmentation

Description: Habitat loss and fragmentation pose a significant threat to leopard populations in Sri Lanka.

Consequences: Leopards face challenges in finding sufficient prey, establishing territories, and maintaining genetic diversity.

Mechanisms to Reduce the Threat:

  • Protected Areas and Corridors
  • Land-Use Planning
  • Community Engagement

Conservation Institution in Sri Lanka link(WWCT): The Wilderness and Wildlife Conservation Trust (WWCT)

Poaching and Illegal Wildlife Trade

Description: Poaching for leopard skins, bones, and other body parts, driven by the illegal wildlife trade, remains a critical threat to leopard populations in Sri Lanka.

Consequences: The poaching of leopards disrupts the natural balance of ecosystems, leads to a decline in leopard numbers, and can result in imbalances in prey populations.

Mechanisms to Reduce the Threat:

  • Anti-Poaching Measures
  • Legislation and Enforcement
  • Community Reporting Systems
